I think at this point, if you are in denial about AI and the change it has brought to this industry, I don't know what to tell you. The models are good. Really good. Probably much better and faster than I will ever be at coding. I personally think this is great. To me, software was never about writing code; it was about building things. Now we can build things faster, and maybe even better than before. Going from red to green is what gives me dopamine.

A couple of years ago, I recall having an issue with the way Jenkins was loading env vars, or something along those lines. The fix was spending a few hours looking at the monolith that is the Jenkins open-source repo to understand how Jenkins constructs them and injects them into Docker containers during build time. All of this was preventing me from actually doing what I wanted, which was getting my CI pipelines working properly.

But now? I could have solved this issue in a matter of minutes. I could just clone the repo locally, give it the pipeline code as context, and then tell either Claude or Codex to go figure out what the problem is. It would scan the repo faster than I ever could, being the mere mortal that I am, and then most likely give me the right answer.

We are writing more software than ever. Sure, do we get AI slop? Yeah. But I'd rather have an internal tool that makes my life slightly easier than not have it at all. I can finally build the endless side projects I've always wanted to build because I no longer have to spend time researching how to make them. I just need to think about what to build.

Not everyone is on board with this, and not everyone shares my optimism. That's fine. I don't think they need to, but anyone who is still saying AI can't write code doesn't know what they are talking about.

I think processes like Agile and the various rituals that accompany it are becoming less useful by the day. What does it even mean to estimate a story anymore? Claude can probably write the entire feature by the time you finish estimating how long it is going to take. Code has become cheap, and it makes no sense to argue about the minutiae of whether you should write a feature one way or the other. You can probably try both and see what works.

The other thing I noticed is that we are somehow generating more documentation, and better-quality documentation, all in the name of "context" for the model. Who could have guessed that context for the model also really helps developers? I've seen more documentation written in the past few months than I ever have in my life writing software. It always felt like a chore to go update the Swagger spec or some other documentation. Now you can just have Claude do it for you.

Maybe I'm too optimistic. Maybe I don't know what I'm talking about. Maybe all of this is unsustainable, and we're going to see an increase in incidents from tech companies, not that we aren't already, as both Claude and GitHub can barely maintain two nines apparently. But think about this: are we really going to go back? The toothpaste is out of the tube, and there's no putting it back in. I think even if we stopped all development and training for LLMs today, we'd still be using them, because even a 15% productivity gain is enough to justify it, and this is clearly more than that.
